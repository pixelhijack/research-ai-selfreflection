## Summary: brief recap of the main findings

This research investigates the emerging use of AI as a tool for **self-reflection** and psychological engagement, highlighting its role as a novel space distinct from traditional therapy due to its immediacy and low social cost. Key findings from the study include:

- **Emotional Disclosure and Accessibility:** Approximately **56% of participants use AI for emotional disclosure**, driven by the absence of social judgment and the tool's 24/7 availability. While only 12.5% of the total sample views AI as a substitute for therapy, those facing **accessibility barriers** (such as cost or waitlists) are 4.3 times more likely to frame AI as a therapeutic replacement.
- **Impact on Neurodivergent (ND) Users:** Neurodivergent individuals, who made up 54% of the sample, report slightly higher rates of emotional disclosure (59%) compared to neurotypical users (52%). While their overall frequency of AI use is similar to neurotypicals, **ND users engage with AI across a broader range of personal problem domains** and frequently use it to help navigate executive function challenges.
- **The Power of Dissonant Feedback:** The study's most robust finding is that **93.7% of users who received challenging or "dissonant" feedback from an AI reported improved self-understanding**. Rather than defensively rejecting contradictory information, users tend to engage with it, suggesting that the low-stakes nature of AI facilitates meaningful self-concept change.
- **Cognitive Offloading:** Most users (77.8%) treat AI as a **"metacognitive scaffold" or an external thinking tool**. Its primary psychological use cases are brainstorming (61.1%) and obtaining structured overviews of complex problems (53.2%).
- **Trust and Risk Perception:** Users generally prioritize **factual accuracy and misinformation (59.5%)** as their top concern, ranking emotional dependency much lower (31.7%). Interestingly, users often apply heuristic "gut feelings" to **reject** AI output but use more deliberate, content-based criteria to accept it.
- **Identification of Sycophancy:** Qualitative results show that sophisticated users have identified **sycophancy (the AI's tendency to simply agree with the user)** as a more dangerous risk to genuine self-reflection than simple hallucinations.
