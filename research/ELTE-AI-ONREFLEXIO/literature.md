# 1. Human–AI Interaction (HAI)

## What this field studies

- How people perceive, anthropomorphize, and emotionally respond to AI systems
- Trust calibration in automated systems
- Social responses to machines
- Conversational agent design
- Parasocial and relational dynamics with non-human agents

It overlaps with HCI, social psychology, and communication science.

---

## Common findings

- People treat machines socially even when they know they are machines.
- Anthropomorphic framing increases perceived warmth and trust.
- Trust in AI often exceeds actual system reliability.
- Users disclose more to conversational agents when they perceive low social threat.
- Emotional bonding can emerge even with simple agents.

Directionally:
AI is not processed purely as a tool. It activates social cognition systems.

---

## Internal debates

- Is anthropomorphism a design trick or an emergent human bias?
- Does relational framing increase effectiveness or dependency?
- Should emotional AI simulate empathy?

There’s a tension between:

> AI as instrument
> vs
> AI as social actor

That maps directly onto your “AI-as-tool vs AI-as-agent” hypothesis.

---

## What’s missing

- Naturalistic self-reflection use cases (most studies are lab-based)
- Long-term cognitive impact
- Neurodivergence-specific interaction patterns
- Non-clinical, non-task AI usage

Most research studies task completion, not existential processing.

---

## How this fits your hypotheses

Supports:

- H1 (self-disclosure)
- H4 (pseudo-therapeutic substitution)
- H5 (tool vs agent clusters)

Your study contributes by:
Examining spontaneous, organic AI use rather than engineered chatbot trials.

---

## Where to search

Search in:

- Google Scholar
- PubMed
- ACM Digital Library
- PsycINFO

Keywords:

- “human-AI interaction trust”
- “anthropomorphism AI”
- “social responses to computers”
- “conversational agents emotional disclosure”

Avoid:
Purely technical ML papers.

# 2. Self-Disclosure Online

## What this field studies

- Why people disclose more online
- Anonymity effects
- Online disinhibition
- Emotional vulnerability in digital spaces
- Text-based intimacy

---

## Common findings

- Anonymity increases depth of disclosure.
- Reduced nonverbal cues reduce social anxiety.
- Asynchronous communication increases reflection quality.
- People often report feeling safer disclosing to strangers online.

Directionally:
Lower social risk → higher introspective depth.

---

## Internal debates

- Does online disclosure improve well-being or reinforce rumination?
- Is anonymity protective or destabilizing?
- Are digital disclosures authentic or curated?

---

## What’s missing

- AI-specific disclosure (most studies are human-to-human online)
- Difference between human anonymity and AI non-judgment
- Neurodivergent populations’ differential comfort

---

## How this fits your hypotheses

Strong anchor for:

- H1 (greater self-disclosure to AI)
- H2 (ND modulation due to reduced social load)

You extend the field from:
Human-human → human-AI disclosure.

---

## Where to search

Keywords:

- “online disinhibition effect”
- “computer-mediated self-disclosure”
- “text-based therapy disclosure”
- “anonymity and emotional expression”

Fields:
Communication research journals
Cyberpsychology journals

# 3. Cognitive Offloading & Extended Mind

This is your strongest theoretical pillar.

## What this field studies

- Using tools to extend cognitive processes
- Memory offloading (Google effect)
- External scaffolding
- Distributed cognition
- Metacognitive aids

---

## Common findings

- People offload memory to devices when available.
- External structuring tools increase task organization.
- Writing increases metacognitive clarity.
- Tools can reduce cognitive load but may weaken internal retention.

Directionally:
Externalization reorganizes internal cognition.

---

## Internal debates

- Does offloading reduce cognitive ability long-term?
- Or does it free resources for higher-level thinking?
- Where is the boundary of “mind”?

---

## What’s missing

- AI as dynamic cognitive partner (not static tool)
- AI in emotional processing, not just memory
- ND-specific executive scaffolding through AI

Most offloading research predates LLMs.

# 4. Digital Mental Health Tools

## What this field studies

- Therapy chatbots
- CBT apps
- AI-based mood trackers
- Online therapy effectiveness
- Therapeutic alliance with digital agents

---

## Common findings

- Digital CBT tools show mild-to-moderate effectiveness.
- Therapeutic alliance can form with chatbots.
- Dropout rates are high.
- Engagement predicts outcomes more than tool sophistication.

Directionally:
Digital tools can help — but are not equivalent to therapy.

---

## Internal debates

- Are chatbots ethical for vulnerable populations?
- Does simulated empathy mislead users?
- Can AI replace therapists?

---

## What’s missing

- Non-clinical exploratory self-use
- Identity-based usage patterns (ND vs NT)
- Spontaneous AI reflection outside designed CBT apps

Your survey likely captures:
Unregulated, self-directed AI usage.

That’s novel.

---

## How this fits your hypotheses

Anchors:

- H4 (therapy substitution)
- Risk calibration
- Emotional reliance questions

You position your work as:
Pre-clinical landscape mapping.
