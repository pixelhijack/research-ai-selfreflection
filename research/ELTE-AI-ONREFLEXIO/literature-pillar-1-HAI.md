# Pillar#1: HUMAN-AI CONNECTION

## HAI: Lombard & Xu (2021): MASA (Media Are Social Actors) framework

This academic text introduces the **Media Are Social Actors (MASA) paradigm**, a theoretical framework designed to explain how humans psychologically respond to **artificial intelligence and social robots**. It serves as a modern extension of the classic **Computers Are Social Actors (CASA)** model, addressing how contemporary technologies evoke a sense of **social presence**. The authors categorize **social cues** into primary and secondary groups, suggesting that a hierarchy of these signals determines the strength of a user’s interaction. Through various studies, the research explores the tension between **mindless and mindful anthropomorphism**, investigating why people instinctively apply human social norms to machines. Ultimately, the source provides **formal propositions** and future research directions to help designers and scholars navigate the evolving relationship between humans and **autonomous entities**.

---

The research primarily focuses on the transition from the **Computers Are Social Actors (CASA)** paradigm to the **Media Are Social Actors (MASA)** paradigm, providing empirical evidence for how humans interact with modern AI-driven technologies.

### Key Findings of the Research

- **Social Responses to Modern Media:** Humans continue to apply social norms and scripts to a wide range of modern technologies, including **social robots and smartphones**, treating them as social actors rather than mere tools.
- **Hierarchy of Social Cues:** Not all social cues are equal; there is a hierarchy where **facial and kinetic (movement) cues** have a stronger effect on evoking a sense of social presence than vocal or linguistic cues.
- **Dominance of Mindlessness:** The research finds that **mindlessness**—an involuntary and effortless psychological process—is a more powerful explanation for why people respond socially to technology than mindful, conscious anthropomorphism.
- **Moderating Factors:** Social responses are not universal; they are influenced by **individual differences** (such as prior experience with robots or general attitudes toward them) and the **communication context** (such as whether the interaction is task-oriented or socio-emotional).
- **Moral and Legal Shifts:** As AI becomes more human-like, human moral reasoning shifts from seeing machines as property to considering **"quasi-animal rights"** and broader societal responsibilities, particularly when witnessing "abuse" toward robots.

### Key Terms and Explanations

- **CASA (Computers Are Social Actors):** A theoretical framework suggesting that individuals inadvertently and mindlessly respond to desktop computers as if they were human beings.
- **MASA (Media Are Social Actors):** An updated and more structured paradigm designed to explain social responses to the broader spectrum of 21st-century technologies, such as virtual agents, social robots, and AR.
- **Medium-as-Social-Actor Presence:** A psychological state where a user experiences a **"perceptual illusion of non-mediation,"** treating a technology or artificial actor as if it were a real person.
- **Primary Cues:** Highly salient features central to the human perception of "socialness," such as **eye gaze, gestures, and human-sounding voices**.
- **Secondary Cues:** Less central features that can still evoke social responses but are not sufficient on their own, such as **machine-sounding voices, language, and size**.
- **Mindless Anthropomorphism:** A psychological process where individuals are **oblivious to the asocial nature** of a machine and automatically respond to its social cues based on long-term habits.
- **Mindful Anthropomorphism:** A state where individuals **actively and thoughtfully** assign human characteristics to a non-human agent, often seen in children playing with toys.

### Relevant Citations for Research on "AI Usage and Self-Reflection"

While the sources do not focus on general human self-reflection, they offer significant insights into how users **reflect on the nature of the AI** they interact with:

- **Mindful vs. Mindless Processing:** To understand how users reflect on AI sociality, you can cite **Xu, Chen, & Huang (2022)**, who used multidimensional scaling to compare cognitive maps in mindful (where users were asked to reflect and code cues) versus mindless conditions.
- **Qualitative Moral Reflection:** For research on how anthropomorphism triggers deeper moral reflection, **Yang et al. (2025)** provide findings on "qualitative reflections" that show how people shift their reasoning from technical assessments to character-based condemnation when viewing robot abuse.
- **Cognitive Processing Routes:** **Lombard & Xu (2021)** and **Nass & Moon (2000)** provide the theoretical basis for "mindful anthropomorphism," which involves a "thoughtful, sincere belief" in a machine's human characteristics—a core component of how a user might self-reflect on their AI usage.
- **Suspension of Disbelief:** **Duffy & Zawieska (2012)** discuss the "suspension of disbelief" in social robotics, which is a relevant concept for how users consciously engage in a "balance between fantasy and realism" during AI interaction.
